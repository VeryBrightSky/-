Topical Finasteride, Explained Without the Hype

3 min read · The same DHT-blocking drug as the pill, applied to the scalp instead. Here's what's proven, what's promising, and what's marketing. · reader-supported, partner links marked (details)

How finasteride works in the first place

Male pattern hair loss is driven mostly by DHT (dihydrotestosterone), a hormone that shrinks genetically sensitive follicles until they stop producing visible hair. Finasteride blocks the enzyme that converts testosterone into DHT. The 1mg oral tablet has been FDA-approved for this since 1997, and it remains one of the two treatments with the deepest evidence base (the other is minoxidil).

Why put it on your scalp instead?

The pill lowers DHT throughout your entire body. That's why it works, and it's also why a small percentage of men report sexual side effects such as lowered libido or erectile dysfunction. Topical finasteride is an attempt to keep the benefit and shrink the risk: deliver the drug where the follicles are, with less of it circulating in your blood. Research so far supports two claims: topical formulations reduce scalp DHT while producing much lower blood levels of the drug than the pill, and studies to date show hair count improvements in a similar ballpark to oral treatment. The catch: the body of evidence is far smaller and younger than the decades of data behind the pill.

Key fact most blogs bury: topical finasteride is not FDA-approved. It's a compounded medication prescribed off-label at a provider's discretion, which is legal and common, but it means formulations vary between pharmacies and there's no single standardized product.

How people actually get it

Because it's compounded and prescription-only, you get topical finasteride through a dermatologist or a telehealth service working with compounding pharmacies. The best-known options: Happy Head, which is dermatologist-run and compounds the strongest combinations we've seen (finasteride or dutasteride with up to 8% minoxidil, ~$71 to $79/month, reviewed here), and Hims and Keeps, which offer standard-strength topical fin/minoxidil combos at lower prices (compared here and here).

Whichever route you pick, the rules are the same: results take 3-6 months, early shedding is normal, and stopping treatment means losing the gains. Pick the option you can afford and stick with for years, not the one with the best ad.
